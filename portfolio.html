Introduction:

In 2018, the public was drawn to Cambridge Analytica, which used a Facebook app called âThis Is Your Digital Lifeâ to collect massive amounts of personal information. Marketed as a simple personality quiz, the tool gathered data from individuals who took the quiz and their unsuspecting friends, thanks to Facebookâs lax API permissions at the time (ICO, 2018). These data points fed into psychographic models, enabling the microtargeting of political ads during the 2016 US Presidential Election and the UKâs Brexit referendum.

From an ethical standpoint, the scandal underscored a serious breach of informed consent. Many Facebook users were unaware that their information could be shared so extensively. Legally, the episode prompted tighter regulatory scrutiny and hefty fines for Facebook, paving the way for more rigorous enforcement of data protection laws such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). Societally, it raised alarms about the potential for social media to undermine democratic processes through subtle, data-driven persuasion (The Guardian, 2018).

Not long after, TikTok became the focal point of a parallel controversy. While many of TikTokâs quizzes appear innocuousâthink âWhich Animal Matches Your Personality?ââdeeper investigation showed they sometimes request data like location, device information, and browsing habits (EDPB, 2023). Beyond the legal ramifications, TikTokâs quizzes raise essential ethical concerns around transparency and consent. This lack of transparency fosters mistrust, damaging the platformâs reputation and broader public confidence in technology companies.

Both cases have illustrated the dangers of poorly disclosed data collection practices. Informed consent must be explicitly taken, and users should understand how their data will be used and processed, stored, and potentially shared, any unauthorised data exploitation can erode public trust, polarising political discourse, and normalise privacy infringements in particular among young audiences who rely heavily on social media. We must mention that Legal Challenges such as GDPR have been introduced to control data misuse. Still, enforcement often struggles to keep up with evolving technology, and firms caught breaching these laws face substantial penalties and reputational damage and are professionally obligated to respect user privacy. The ACM Code of Ethics (2018) and similar frameworks emphasise transparency, security, and user welfare over commercial or political advantage.

So in conclusion, the controversies surrounding Cambridge Analytica and TikTok highlight how seemingly trivial quizzes can be conduits for widespread data collection and manipulation. Transparency is paramount: organisations must communicate their data practices and prioritise privacy, mainly when catering to younger users. While regulatory bodies have begun imposing more significant fines and stricter oversight, the technological landscape continues to evolve rapidly, making it essential for companies to integrate privacy and ethics into their core operations. F-Test and t-Test Analysis

A. F-Test: Two-Sample for Variances

The F-test helps determine whether the variances of the two groups (male and female incomes) are significantly different.

Key Results:

  1. F-statistic: 1.22586

This value represents the ratio of the variance for males (233.129) to the variance for females (190.176).

  1. p-value (one-tail): 0.2182

This indicates the probability of observing an F-statistic at least as extreme as 1.22586 if the null hypothesis of equal variances is true.

  1. F Critical (one-tail): 1.53996

The critical value at a 5% significance level, given 59 degrees of freedom in each group.

Interpretation:

Since F < Fcritical (1.2259 < 1.5400) and p > 0.05 (0.2182 > 0.05), we do not reject the null hypothesis. Therefore, there is no statistically significant evidence that the variances of male and female incomes differ. This result justifies assuming equal variances for the subsequent t-test.

B. t-Test: Two-Sample Assuming Equal Variances

Given the F-test results, the t-test examines whether the mean male income is significantly higher than the mean female income under the assumption of equal variances.

Key Results:

  1. Means:

— Males: 52.913
— Females: 44.233
— Difference in means: 52.913 − 44.233 ≈ 8.68

  1. t-Statistic: 3.2679

— Reflects the standardized difference between the two means, using the pooled variance.

  1. Degrees of Freedom (df): 118

— Derived from the sample sizes and variances in both groups.

  1. p-value (one-tail): 0.00071

— The probability of observing a t-statistic at least as large as 3.2679 under the null hypothesis.

  1. t Critical (one-tail): 1.6579

— The threshold at Î± = 0.05 for a one-tailed test.

Interpretation:

Since t > tcritical (3.2679 > 1.6579) and p < 0.05 (0.00071 < 0.05), we reject the null hypothesis. This indicates strong evidence that the mean male income is higher than the mean female income.
